Metal Roofing vs. Asphalt Shingles: Which Is the Better Investment?

Your roof replacement decision will shape your home for decades. With material costs, labor, and energy bills all in the mix, choosing between metal roofing and asphalt shingles means weighing upfront investment against long-term value—and getting that balance right matters.

It's a dilemma most Minneapolis-St. Paul homeowners face when their current roof starts showing its age. Asphalt shingles dominate the residential market because of their lower entry cost, but metal roofing has earned serious ground among homeowners who prioritize durability and energy performance.

The right call comes down to your budget, how long you plan to stay in the home, your home's style, and what you want out of the investment. Here's a straightforward breakdown of the factors that should drive your decision.

Upfront Cost Analysis

Asphalt Shingles: The Budget-Friendly Option

Asphalt shingles typically run $3.50 to $7.00 per square foot installed, making them the most accessible option for most homeowners. On a standard 2,000 square foot roof, expect to invest somewhere between $7,000 and $14,000 for a full replacement.

That lower price point comes from mass production and wide availability. Three-tab shingles are the most economical choice, while architectural shingles step up in durability and appearance for a modest premium.

Metal Roofing: Higher Initial Investment

Metal roofing carries a significantly larger upfront cost—typically $8.00 to $16.00 per square foot installed. That same 2,000 square foot roof would run $16,000 to $32,000 depending on the metal type and profile.

Standing seam sits at the higher end of that range, offering superior weather resistance and a clean, modern look. Corrugated metal panels offer a more budget-friendly entry point while delivering most of metal's key benefits.

The price difference comes from both the materials themselves and the specialized installation process. Metal roofing requires contractors who know proper fastening methods and understand thermal expansion—this isn't work for just any roofer.

Lifespan and Durability Comparison

Asphalt Shingle Longevity

Quality asphalt shingles last 20 to 30 years with proper upkeep. Architectural shingles tend to outlast three-tab varieties by 5 to 10 years, thanks to their thicker construction and better wind resistance.

Minnesota's winters are hard on shingles. Ice dams, hail, and UV exposure gradually break down the material—leading to granule loss, cracking, and eventual failure. The climate here shortens lifespans that might be longer in milder regions.

Metal Roofing Durability Advantages

Metal roofing systems commonly last 40 to 70 years, with some premium products backed by 50-year warranties. The material doesn't crack, split, or shed granules the way asphalt does over time.

Properly coated steel handles Minnesota weather well. Snow slides off before it can melt and refreeze at the gutters—dramatically reducing ice dam formation. Most metal roofing carries a Class A fire rating and withstands winds up to 140 mph. Aluminum or copper extends longevity even further—a well-maintained copper roof often outlasts the house beneath it.

Energy Efficiency and Climate Performance

Asphalt Shingle Thermal Properties

Standard asphalt shingles absorb solar heat all day long. On a sunny afternoon, dark-colored shingles can reach surface temperatures above 150°F—and your air conditioner feels every degree of it. Cool roof shingles with reflective granules help somewhat, but they can't match metal's energy performance.

Metal Roofing Energy Benefits

Metal roofing reflects anywhere from 25% to 95% of solar radiation, depending on the color and coating you choose. Light-colored metal roofs can cut cooling costs by 10% to 25% compared to traditional asphalt—a difference you'll notice on your energy bills.

Metal's low thermal mass means it cools down quickly when temperatures drop—perfect for Minnesota's wild temperature swings. Some metal systems work with additional insulation layers too, boosting energy performance even more.

Maintenance Requirements

Asphalt Shingle Maintenance Needs

Shingles demand regular checkups, especially after storms. You'll need to replace loose or damaged pieces quickly to prevent water damage. Gutters collect granules as shingles wear down, creating more cleaning work. Moss and algae love humid conditions and can speed up deterioration if left untreated.

Then there's the big one: most asphalt roofs need complete replacement every 20 to 30 years—a major expense that hits repeatedly over the decades.

Metal Roofing Maintenance Advantages

A well-installed metal roof asks for very little. You'll want annual checkups to look at fasteners, check sealants around vents and chimneys, and make sure gutters are working properly.

Leaves and debris slide right off the smooth surface on their own, and unlike some other materials, metal simply doesn't give moss, algae, or pests anything to work with. When you're weighing that higher upfront cost, this is one of the most compelling reasons to take it seriously.

Aesthetic Considerations and Home Value Impact

Asphalt Shingle Design Flexibility

Modern asphalt shingles come in dozens of colors, profiles, and textures. Architectural shingles can mimic wood shakes, slate, or tile for a fraction of what those materials would cost. They blend into most neighborhoods without raising eyebrows, keeping buyer appeal strong and property values stable.

Metal Roofing Visual Appeal

That old barn-roof stereotype? It's been outdated for years. Today's standing seam profiles offer clean, contemporary lines that work beautifully with both modern builds and traditional architecture. You'll find colors ranging from warm earth tones to bold contemporary shades, and stone-coated metal tiles can replicate the look of conventional materials while delivering all of metal's performance benefits.

Of course, some HOAs still restrict metal roofing, and the look isn't right for every home or neighborhood.

Resale Value and Return on Investment

Market Perception Factors

A new roof—no matter what material—typically boosts resale value. Most roofing investments recoup 60% to 70% of their cost at sale, though local market conditions and material choice both play a role.

Asphalt shingles have broad buyer appeal. Most buyers are familiar with them, understand the replacement timeline, and can factor that into their evaluation without much friction.

Metal Roofing Premium Value

Metal roofing tends to command a higher resale premium. Buyers who understand the material recognize the long-term savings on maintenance and energy, and the extended lifespan means they won't be facing a roof replacement anytime soon. For value-conscious buyers, that's a meaningful selling point.

Energy efficiency and sustainability matter more to buyers now than ever—trends that favor metal roofing.

Climate-Specific Considerations for Minnesota

Winter Weather Performance

Minnesota winters test every roofing material to its limits. Heavy snow loads, constant freeze-thaw cycles, and ice dam pressure expose weaknesses quickly.

Metal's smooth surface and heat conductivity help snow slide off before it can melt and refreeze at the gutters—which is where most ice dams start. The material also handles heavy snow loads without sagging or putting stress on your home's structure.

Shingles have a tougher time preventing ice dams, especially on complex rooflines with valleys and dormers. If you go with shingles in our climate, proper attic insulation and ventilation aren't just recommended—they're absolutely necessary.

Hail Resistance Comparison

Metal roofing handles hail storms better than shingles. Steel at the right gauge can absorb impacts without permanent damage. Class 4 impact-resistant shingles offer decent protection, but severe storms can still leave cosmetic damage that hurts curb appeal and resale value, even when the roof keeps doing its job.

Installation Considerations and Timeline

Asphalt Shingle Installation

Most shingle jobs wrap up in one to three days, weather permitting. The process is familiar to most roofing crews, keeping labor costs reasonable and scheduling straightforward. Qualified installers are plentiful, reducing project complications.

Metal Roofing Installation Requirements

Metal roofing typically takes three to five days and requires contractors who really know what they're doing. Getting the fastening techniques right, allowing for thermal expansion, and nailing the flashing details—these aren't skills every roofer has. Finding qualified contractors can be trickier in some areas, which might stretch your timeline and bump up costs. But proper installation is what protects your warranty and ensures the roof performs for decades.

Making the Right Choice for Your Situation

When Asphalt Shingles Make Sense

If your budget is tight or you're planning to sell within the next 10 years, asphalt shingles are likely the smarter financial move. The lower upfront cost can deliver better returns over a shorter ownership window.

Homes in neighborhoods with strong aesthetic preferences for traditional materials—or those governed by restrictive covenants—are also well-suited to quality architectural shingles. Broad contractor availability makes project management simpler too.

When Metal Roofing Justifies the Investment

Metal roofing makes the most sense if you're planning to stay put for the long haul. The durability, energy savings, and minimal maintenance over 20-plus years can more than make up for that higher upfront cost.

If your home faces severe weather regularly—hail, high winds, fire risk—metal's superior protection becomes even more valuable. And if you care about environmental impact, metal's energy efficiency and decades-long lifespan are hard to beat.
