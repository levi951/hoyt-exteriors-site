Siding Replacement Cost Guide: What Homeowners Should Expect to Pay

Understanding Siding Replacement Costs

New siding transforms your home like few other improvements can. You get an instant curb appeal upgrade, better weather protection, and real value when it's time to sell.

Most homeowners invest between $8,000 and $25,000 on siding replacement, though your actual costs will vary based on materials, home size, and project complexity. Understanding these cost drivers helps you set a realistic budget and have informed conversations with contractors.

Primary Factors That Drive Siding Costs

Home Size and Square Footage

Contractors typically price siding by the square foot, so your home's exterior surface area is the single biggest cost driver. A typical ranch home covers 1,200–1,500 square feet, while two-story houses usually fall between 2,000 and 2,500 square feet.

To get a rough estimate, measure each wall's height and width, then subtract windows and doors. Keep in mind that dormers, bay windows, and decorative features increase both material needs and installation time.

Material Selection

Your siding material choice has the biggest impact on your final cost.

Vinyl Siding: $3–$8 per square foot installed

Vinyl leads the market because it's affordable, low-maintenance, and comes in dozens of colors and textures. Higher-end vinyl with realistic wood grain or stone textures costs more initially but creates a much more upscale look.

Fiber Cement Siding: $6–$12 per square foot installed

This blend of cement, sand, and cellulose mimics wood's appearance while resisting fire, insects, and rot. You'll need to repaint every 10–15 years, but quality fiber cement can last 50 years or more.

Wood Siding: $8–$20 per square foot installed

Cedar, pine, and engineered wood create natural warmth and character, though they require regular maintenance. Whether you choose basic pine or premium cedar shakes makes a huge difference in your total cost.

Steel and Aluminum Siding: $5–$11 per square foot installed

Metal siding offers excellent durability and fire resistance. While less popular for homes, it works beautifully for contemporary designs and commercial buildings.

Stone and Brick Veneer: $12–$25 per square foot installed

Natural stone and manufactured veneer make stunning facades, though they need expert installation and structural modifications to handle the extra weight.

Labor and Installation Complexity

Labor accounts for 40–60% of most project budgets, with certain factors driving that percentage even higher.

Architectural Features

Multi-story homes, dormers, bay windows, and intricate trim work require more time and specialized skills. Every corner, angle, and transition adds hours to the installation.

Existing Siding Removal

Removing old siding typically costs $1–$3 per square foot. Homes with asbestos siding require specialized removal procedures that increase costs significantly.

Structural Repairs

After removing old siding, contractors often discover rotted sheathing, damaged framing, or insufficient insulation. Smart homeowners budget an extra 10–20% for unexpected repairs.

Access and Site Conditions

Steep rooflines, tight spaces, or landscaping that limits equipment access can slow a crew down and may result in additional charges.

Regional Cost Variations

Expect price differences across markets. Around Minneapolis–St. Paul, labor runs higher than in rural areas, though material costs stay pretty consistent statewide.

Cold climates bring their own installation challenges—thermal expansion requirements and moisture management details that bump up labor costs. The upside? Local contractors know these challenges inside and out. Building codes play a role too. Permits, inspections, and material restrictions (especially in historic districts or fire zones) can surprise you with both budget and timeline impacts.

Getting Accurate Siding Quotes

Preparing for Contractor Meetings

Good preparation makes contractor meetings much more productive. Gather photos of siding styles you like, decide which materials match your priorities, and research warranty options ahead of time. Making sure contractors can easily walk around your home's perimeter also helps them give you more accurate estimates.

What Professional Estimates Should Include

A solid siding quote breaks things down clearly:

  • Material costs with specific product names and quantities
  • Labor charges for removal, prep, and installation
  • Permit and inspection fees
  • Waste disposal and cleanup
  • Project timeline with start and completion dates
  • Warranty details for both materials and workmanship

If one bid comes in dramatically lower than the others, that's worth questioning. Unusually low estimates often mean something is being cut. Inflated bids can signal inefficiency. Either way, you want to understand what's behind the number.

Questions to Ask Potential Contractors

Licensing and Insurance

Ask for current licenses and proof of comprehensive insurance. Get the certificates and verify them directly with the provider.

Experience and References

Find out whether the contractor has worked with your specific material and home style before. Ask for references from recent projects and, if possible, the chance to see completed work in person.

Installation Timeline

Get a realistic sense of how long the project will take. Weather delays happen, but a good contractor communicates schedule changes promptly.

Warranty Coverage

Understand exactly what's covered under material warranties versus workmanship warranties, and know how to file a claim for each.

Cost-Saving Strategies

Timing Your Project

Late fall through early spring tends to be slower for siding contractors, and many offer discounts during that stretch to keep crews busy. Just be careful not to schedule during stretches of extreme cold or heat—those conditions can compromise installation quality. Spring and fall tend to be the sweet spot, offering reasonable pricing alongside weather that's actually cooperative.

Material Selection Trade-offs

Mid-grade materials often deliver 90% of the performance of premium options at a meaningfully lower price—and many vinyl and fiber cement products land right in that range.

You can also be deliberate about where you put your dollars. Spending more on the front facade, where curb appeal is most visible, while going with more economical options on the sides and back is a practical way to manage costs without giving up the look you're after.

Partial Replacement Options

Full replacement isn't always necessary. When your existing siding remains structurally sound, fresh trim, shutters, or accent panels can revitalize the appearance for much less money. Many homeowners start with the most weathered sections, then tackle remaining areas over the next few years to spread out the expense.

Additional Costs to Consider

Insulation Upgrades

Siding replacement gives you an ideal chance to boost your home's energy efficiency. House wrap and foam board insulation add $1–$3 per square foot but create real savings in heating and cooling costs while improving comfort.

Trim and Accent Work

Fresh siding typically calls for updated trim around windows, doors, and rooflines. Depending on the material and complexity, budget $3–$8 per linear foot for trim replacement.

Gutter and Downspout Coordination

If your gutters need attention, handling that at the same time as your siding project is more cost-effective than scheduling them separately. New gutters installed after siding completion typically cost more.

Permits and Inspections

Most municipalities require permits for siding replacement, typically running $100–$500. Depending on where you live, you may also run into energy efficiency inspections or historical district approvals that add both time and expense to the process.

Return on Investment

Homeowners who invest in quality siding replacement generally recover 70–80% of the cost at resale—putting it among the stronger exterior upgrades from a financial standpoint. In competitive neighborhoods with premium materials, that return can climb even higher.

The financial benefits extend beyond resale value. Fresh siding reduces maintenance headaches, helps control energy costs, and protects your home's structure. These advantages build over time, making the initial investment look smarter with each passing year.

Working with Professional Contractors

Your siding project's success hinges on contractors who understand local codes, know their materials, and have real experience with Minnesota's climate. Our dramatic temperature swings require specific installation techniques that inexperienced crews often miss.

Quality installation ensures proper moisture barriers, correct thermal expansion allowances, and full warranty compliance—factors that determine how your siding performs and looks for years to come.

Making Your Decision

Siding replacement represents a major investment that influences your home's protection, efficiency, and curb appeal for decades. Research your options thoroughly, gather multiple detailed quotes, and verify contractor credentials before moving forward.

Your timeline for the property matters too. If you're planning to sell within five years, lean toward materials with broad appeal and solid cost-effectiveness. If you're staying long-term, premium materials with lower maintenance requirements and better durability are worth the added upfront cost.
